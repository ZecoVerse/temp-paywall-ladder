Zero Shot #6

The gold-rush-shovel-window and deus ex machina

By Rohin Dharmakumar

Predicaments for generative AI through an analogy overload

15 Oct 2025  /  6 min read

  Comment

Rohin is Co-founder and CEO at The Ken. He hosts The Ken's leadership podcast, First Principles, and is the lead writer of the Sunday newsletter on mental models, careers and personal growth, also called First Principles.

Always find us faster: mark The Ken as your go-to source on Google Search, we'll pop up first when you search for relevant information.

Hello and welcome back to Zero Shot. I feel like there’s a time dilation effect playing out with multiple Zero Shot clocks, me, and you:

Clock 1: Time elapsed = 18 days

I last wrote the column, Edition 3 (“China and the AI puck”), on 27 September. And now I’m writing today, on 15 October.

Clock 2: Time elapsed = 4 days

Because, in the interim, my colleagues Brady and Praveen wrote their respective columns on 4 October (“The Slop King and his Subjects”) and 11 October (“ChatGPT brings UPI full circle to credit cards and wallets”).

Clock 3: Time elapsed = 0 days

Because there’s a new episode of the Zero Shot podcast out today!

And interestingly, the only two appropriate movie scene analogies both feature Matthew McConaughey—from True Detective and Interstellar!

The gold-rush-shovel-window and deus ex machina

The GenAI “revolution” (when will it be okay to stop using the quotes? Never? Okay.) is built on top of language. Thus, it’s only fitting that we use analogies to understand its effects.

But the sheer scale and unpredictability of GenAI means that no single analogy can suffice, so I’ve brought three!

Analogy 1: Shovels during a gold rush

This is itself a composite analogy, consisting of both the California Gold Rush of 1848–1855 and the counterintuitive wisdom of making money by viewing miners, not gold, as the opportunity (see Samuel Brannan).

Sadly, this analogy has been done to death with the current AI boom, because the comparisons are obvious! The Samuel Brannan of our age is Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang. Oracle founder Larry Ellison is a close second.

The most interesting companies are going upstream to sell to AI giants, not downstream to sell to consumers. Because, (a) not enough consumers are paying, and (b) deep pockets and desperation, the unbeatable combination trait that makes for dream customers, are now in large AI giants.

But at some point, even the shovel sellers realise their customers are in way over their heads, which is when they start loaning money to those customers who use it to buy more shovels. 

Analogy 2: Are the windows disappearing or the walls closing?

Now, a “window of opportunity is a short, often fleeting period during which a rare and desired action can be taken. Once the window closes, the opportunity may never come again.”

A key thrust of the GenAI “revolution” has been the hope for an all-powerful AGI—artificial general intelligence—a human-level intelligence that can do most of the work we can do.

But talk of AGI has diminished during the last six months. One reason is because more researchers, scientists, and experts are coming to the view that LLMs—the technology that underpins the current GenAI wave—are fundamentally unsuited for AGI.

Of these, the one that must have hurt the most was the admission from Richard Sutton, one of the most respected computer scientists in the AI field, to Dwarkesh Patel that “LLMs are a dead end.” Because Sutton’s 2019 essay “The Bitter Lesson”, which held that throwing more computing power was in general the best way to advance AI systems, was almost gospel for the GenAI faithful.

So, on the one hand, the future (AGI) is becoming hazier through the windows.

On the other hand, the very real and undeniable past is catching up. I mean the very real dollars that have been spent or committed to building data centers.

I read the investor approach of Harris “Kuppy” Kupperman, the founder of hedge fund Praetorian Capital. The math was daunting.

“Last month, I chose to strip away all the hubris around AI and ask one simple question, one that oddly no one had really bothered to ask; how much revenue is needed to justify the current level of capex spend and give AI investors a return on their capital??

…

To start with, I had previously assumed a 10-year depreciation curve, which I now recognize as quite unrealistic based upon the speed with which AI datacenter technology is advancing. Based on my conversations over the past month, the physical datacenters last for three to ten years, at most. Changes to cooling systems, chip and racking designs, power systems, and even overall layouts, mean that the buildings themselves are likely depreciating quite rapidly as well. Then when you consider that new GPU iterations, which seem to come out every year or two, effectively obsolete prior models, you realize that I should have been using a much faster depreciation curve across the whole capitalized structure.

However, if you speed up the depreciation curve to something in the three to five-year range, it would imply that my prior breakeven revenue number of $160 billion to justify 2025’s capex spend, is woefully inadequate. In reality, the industry probably needs a revenue range that is closer to the $320 billion to $480 billion range, just to break even on the capex to be spent this year. As I wasn’t educated on the intricacies of a datacenter, I wasn’t bearish enough on the economics of an AI datacenter. No wonder my new contacts in the industry shoulder a heavy burden—heavier than I could ever imagine. They know the truth.

Remember, the industry is spending over $30 billion a month (approximately $400 billion for 2025) and only receiving a bit more than a billion a month back in revenue. The mismatch is astonishing, and this ignores that in 2026, hundreds of billions of additional datacenters will get built, all needing additional revenue to justify their existence. Adding the two years together, and using the math from my prior post, you’d need approximately $1 trillion in revenue to hit break even, and many trillions more to earn an acceptable return on this spend. Remember again, that revenue is currently running at around $15 to $20 billion today.”

Tomasz Tunguz, a venture capitalist, analysed it from more angles—customer base concentration, GPU-backed debt, threat from newer chipsets, complex SPVs, and depreciation.

“Amazon’s 2025 reversal (6 → 5 years) is the first major pullback.

CPUs historically have 5–10 years of useful life , while GPUs in AI datacenters last 1–3 years in practice , despite 6-year accounting assumptions. Evidence from Google architects shows GPUs at 60–70% utilization survive 1–2 years , with 3 years maximum. Meta’s Llama 3 training experienced 9% annual GPU failure rates, suggesting 27% failure over 3 years.

Cerno Capital raises the question : ‘Are these policies a reflection of genuine economic & technological realities? Or are these policies a lever by which hyperscalers are enhancing the optics of their investment programs amid rising investor concerns?’”

The future is becoming hazier. The past is catching up.

Analogy 3: Deus ex machina

Most Indians are familiar with the hand of God. After all, we’ve grown up watching movies in which impossible plot situations are magically resolved with implausible plot twists.

A protagonist’s lost memory suddenly comes back. A child lost in a festival ends up as a stranger in the same house. A new character walks through the door when all seems lost.

Well, a deus ex machina is what the GenAI ecosystem needs right now.

“Deus ex machina (‘god from the machine’) refers to a plot device where an unsolvable problem is suddenly resolved by an unexpected intervention. In the context of AGI, Deus ex machina now has a double meaning: the literal creation of a new godlike intelligence, but also the only path forward to resolve an increasingly tricky ROI problem.”

— David Cahn, “AI CapEx Now Hinges on Deus ex machina”

What is the “killer app” that will generate many hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue in a year, and then multiples thereof, before the capex bills are due?

We don’t know.

GenAI giants are stuck inside massive valuation bubbles inflated through private funding and debt, with greedy vendors on one side and stingy consumers on the other. The time window is closing.

You need something new to reset everything to a new, higher normal. It doesn’t look like AGI. What is it?

“Training data” (Interesting reads)


The AI coding trap

“Software development is fundamentally a practice of problem-solving, and so, as with solving a tricky crossword, most of the work is done in your head.

In the software development lifecycle, coding is the letters filled into the crossword, only a small amount of effort compared to all the head scratching and scribbled notes. The real work usually happens alongside coding, as the developer learns the domain, narrows down requirements, maps out relevant abstractions, considers side effects, tests features incrementally, and finally squashes bugs that survived this rigorous process.

But with AI-driven coding, things play out very differently.

“Code first, ask questions later”

AI coding agents such as Claude Code are making it astonishingly fast to write code in isolation. But most software lives within complex systems, and since LLMs can’t yet hold the full context of an application in memory at once, human review, testing, and integration needs will remain. And that is a lot harder when the code has been written without the human thinking about it. As a result, for complex software, much of the time will be spent on post hoc understanding of what code the AI has written.” — Chris Loy

If The Economist is doing thought experiments about OpenAI going bust, then it’s okay for you to discuss the possibility with your tech friends. Really.

“The ubiquity of Mr Altman and his startup, plus its convoluted links to other AI firms, is raising eyebrows. An awful lot seems to hinge on a firm forecast to lose $10bn this year on revenues of little more than that amount. D.A. Davidson, a broker, calls OpenAI ‘the biggest case yet of Silicon Valley’s vaunted “fake it ’till you make it” ethos’. Only Nvidia is more central to the AI boom, going by the scale of the industry’s incestuous relationships. To understand the model-maker’s centrality to the whole AI enterprise, consider a thought experiment. What if OpenAI went bust?”

From SHGs to MFIs… to LLMs?

“Recent empirical evidence seems to confirm what we sense intuitively about these limits. When researchers systematically tested LLM reasoning capabilities through controlled puzzles, they discovered something profound: As problems grow more complex, these models don’t just struggle but collapse entirely. Even more telling, when complexity increases, they begin to reduce their effort, as if giving up. They find simple solutions but then overthink them, exploring wrong paths.

Perhaps this is a window into the fundamental nature of these systems. They excel at pattern matching within familiar territories but cannot genuinely reason through novel complexity. And social change? It lives entirely in that space of the new and the complex, where contexts shift, where culture matters, where every community brings unprecedented challenges. If these models collapse when moving discs between pegs, how can we trust them with the infinitely more complex work of moving hearts, minds, and systems?” — Gautam John (IDR)

90%

“Is 90% of code going to be written by AI? I don’t know. What I do know is, that for me, on this project, the answer is already yes. I’m part of that growing subset of developers who are building real systems this way.

At the same time, for me, AI doesn’t own the code. I still review every line, shape the architecture, and carry the responsibility for how it runs in production. But the sheer volume of what I now let an agent generate would have been unthinkable even six months ago.

That’s why I’m convinced this isn’t some far-off prediction. It’s already here—just unevenly distributed—and the number of developers working like this is only going to grow.” — Armin Ronacher

 Your place

“My tentative theory is that the systems, incentives, and technologies in modern world have managed to slightly empower (many) individuals, and massively empower large organizations, but at the significant expense of small organizations, whose role in the human societal ecosystem has thus shrunk significantly, with many small organizations either weakening in influence or transitioning to (or absorbed by) large organizations. While this imbalanced system does provide significant material comforts (albeit distributed rather unequally) and some limited feeling of agency, it has led at the level of the individual to feelings of disconnection, alienation, loneliness, and cynicism or pessimism about the ability to influence future events or meet major challenges, except perhaps through the often ruthless competition to become wealthy or influential enough to gain, as an individual, a status comparable to a small or even large organization. And larger organizations have begun to imperfectly step in the void formed by the absence of small communities, providing synthetic social or emotional goods that are, roughly speaking, to more authentic such products as highly processed ‘junk’ food is to more nutritious fare, due to the inherently impersonal nature of such organizations (particularly in the modern era of advanced algorithms and AI, which when left to their own devices tend to exacerbate the trends listed above).”  — Terence Tao

 OpenAI is huge in India. Its models are steeped in caste bias

“An MIT Technology Review investigation finds that caste bias is rampant in OpenAI’s products, including ChatGPT. Though CEO Sam Altman boasted during the launch of GPT-5 in August that India was its second-largest market, we found that both this new model, which now powers ChatGPT, and Sora, OpenAI’s text-to-video generator, exhibit caste bias. This risks entrenching discriminatory views in ways that are currently going unaddressed.” — By Nilesh Christopher (MIT Technology Review)

How the AI Bubble Ate Y Combinator

The program is shorter, startups are pushed to build faster, and just about everyone has an AI company. What could go wrong?

Vibe engineering

“The rise of coding agents—tools like Claude Code (released February 2025), OpenAI’s Codex CLI (April) and Gemini CLI (June) that can iterate on code, actively testing and modifying it until it achieves a specified goal, has dramatically increased the usefulness of LLMs for real-world coding problems.

I’m increasingly hearing from experienced, credible software engineers who are running multiple copies of agents at once, tackling several problems in parallel and expanding the scope of what they can take on. I was skeptical of this at first but I’ve started running multiple agents myself now and it’s surprisingly effective, if mentally exhausting!” — Simon Willison
